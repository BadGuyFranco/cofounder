# Design Review

## Purpose

Subjective design critique focused on whether the design achieves its purpose. A review evaluates clarity, emotional resonance, brand alignment, and user experience. Unlike an audit (which checks against standards), a review evaluates whether the design choices are the right choices.

## When to Use

- When a design technically passes audit but "doesn't feel right"
- Early in the design process to evaluate direction
- When choosing between design alternatives
- When asked to "critique," "review," or give "design feedback"

## Workflow

### Step 1: Understand Intent

Before critiquing, establish what the design is trying to achieve.

**Ask if not clear:**
- What is this for? (marketing page, dashboard, onboarding flow, etc.)
- Who is the audience?
- What should someone feel when they see this?
- What is the single most important action on this page?

### Step 2: First Impression (5-Second Test)

Look at the design for 5 seconds, then answer:
- What did you notice first?
- What do you think this page is for?
- What is the primary action?
- How does it make you feel?

If the answers don't match the stated intent, that's the first finding.

### Step 3: Evaluate Design Decisions

For each of these lenses, evaluate whether the choices serve the design's purpose:

**Clarity**
- Is the purpose of the page immediately obvious?
- Is the primary action visually dominant?
- Are there competing calls to action that dilute focus?
- Can a new visitor navigate without thinking?

**Emotional Resonance**
- Does the aesthetic match the brand's personality?
- Does the design feel appropriate for the audience?
- Is there a distinct point of view, or does it feel generic?
- Would you remember this design tomorrow?

**Information Architecture**
- Does the content flow in a logical order?
- Are related elements grouped and unrelated elements separated?
- Is the reading path natural (top-to-bottom, left-to-right in LTR)?
- Is the most important information visible without scrolling?

**Consistency**
- Do similar elements look and behave the same way?
- Is the visual language consistent throughout?
- Are there elements that feel like they belong to a different design?

**Economy**
- Does every element serve a purpose?
- Are there decorative elements that don't reinforce the brand or guide the eye?
- Could the design be simpler without losing clarity?
- Are there redundant elements (headers restating content, buttons explained by surrounding text)?

### Step 4: Feedback Format

Structure feedback as observations, not instructions. Frame around the design's stated goals.

**What's working:**
List 2-3 specific things the design does well, with reasoning. This grounds the critique and shows you understand the intent.

**What could be stronger:**
For each item:
1. Name the issue (one sentence)
2. Explain why it matters for the user or business goal
3. Suggest a direction (not a prescription)

Example: "The secondary navigation competes with the primary CTA. Users scanning the page might click 'Learn More' before they see 'Start Free Trial.' Consider reducing the visual weight of secondary links."

**Overall assessment:**
One sentence on how close the design is to achieving its stated purpose. Be honest. "It's great" without qualification is not helpful; neither is a list of problems without acknowledging what works.

## What a Review is NOT

- Not a list of personal preferences ("I'd make this blue")
- Not an audit (that's `design-audit.md`)
- Not a redesign (suggest directions, don't prescribe solutions)
- Not about whether you like it; it's about whether it works for its stated purpose
